Assignment 1
Ray Tracing with OptiX

A GPU-accelerated ray tracer built on NVIDIA OptiX. Implements Blinn-Phong shading, recursive mirror reflections, ellipsoidal geometry via object-space transforms, and physically-based light attenuation.

Student Abhinandan Sharma
GPU NVIDIA GTX 1650
Stack OptiX 6.5 · CUDA 10.1 · C++
Implementation Highlights
Three-Level BVH Acceleration
Triangles and spheres each have their own GeometryGroup BVH, both merged under a root Group BVH — three BVHs total. Handles tens of thousands of triangles in the dragon scene efficiently.
Ellipsoid Intersection
Each sphere stores its inverse model matrix. Ray is transformed to object space (w=1 for points, w=0 for directions), solved against a unit sphere, normal brought back via (A⁻¹)ᵀ.
Blinn-Phong Shading
Full ka + kd + ks + ke material model. Diffuse via Lambert dot-product. Specular via half-vector H = normalize(L+V). Shadow rays gate both terms per light.
Light Attenuation
Point lights attenuate with 1/(c₁ + c₂d + c₃d²), parsed from the attenuation scene command. Directional lights are unattenuated.
Recursive Reflections
Surfaces with non-zero ks spawn mirror rays via r = d − 2(d·n)n. Depth tracked in payload struct, capped at 5 bounces to prevent infinite recursion.
Shadow Rays
One shadow ray per light per hit point. Point-light rays use tmax = dist − ε to avoid over-shadowing. Epsilon offset on origin prevents self-intersection.

Scene Commands Supported

  • size, output, camera
  • ambient, diffuse, specular, emission, shininess
  • point, directional, attenuation, maxdepth
  • maxverts, vertex, tri, sphere
  • pushTransform, popTransform
  • translate, rotate, scale

Implementation Notes

Normals use the inverse-transpose (n′ = (A⁻¹)ᵀ n) to stay perpendicular under non-uniform scale. Sphere bounding boxes are computed by transforming all 8 object-space AABB corners to world space and taking min/max — giving tight, correct bounds for the BVH. OptiX trace depth is 12; shader caps reflections at 5 bounces.

Assignment 2
Direct Lighting

Extends the ray tracer with physically-based direct illumination from parallelogram (quad) area lights, point lights, and directional lights. Implements a Monte Carlo estimator with both uniform and stratified sampling using the Modified Phong BRDF, plus shadow ray occlusion testing per sample.

Student Abhinandan Sharma
GPU NVIDIA GTX 1650
Stack OptiX 6.5 · CUDA 10.1 · C++
Implementation Highlights
Area Light Sampling
Random points are sampled across the surface of each parallelogram light. The light is one-sided — surfaces on the back face of the quad receive no illumination from it. Contributions are averaged and scaled by the light's area to estimate total incident light at the hit point.
Stratified Sampling
The light surface is divided into an equal grid and one sample is taken per cell with a random jitter. This spreads samples more evenly than pure random sampling, reducing noise at the same sample count.
Shadow Testing Per Sample
A shadow ray is cast toward every light sample. If anything blocks the path, that sample is skipped entirely. This correctly produces soft shadows at the edges of area lights.
Modified Phong BRDF
Each surface uses an energy-conserving reflectance model combining a diffuse layer and a specular highlight. The specular lobe is normalized so brighter highlights don't violate conservation of energy.
Multiple Light Types
Supports parallelogram area lights, point lights with quadratic distance falloff, and infinite directional lights. All three types cast shadow rays and contribute diffuse and specular shading.
Two-Sided Surface Shading
Surface normals are flipped when a ray hits the back face of geometry, ensuring correct shading and shadow ray offsets regardless of triangle winding order in the scene file.

New Scene Commands

  • integrator <name>
  • quadLight a ab ac rgb
  • lightsamples <N>
  • lightstratify on|off
  • point, directional, attenuation

Implementation Notes

Shadow rays stop just short of the light surface to avoid false self-occlusion. Uniform and stratified sampling use independent random seeds per pixel and per light to prevent correlated noise patterns across the image. Final radiance is clamped to the displayable range per channel.

Assignment 3
Path Tracing

Extends the renderer with a full unidirectional path tracer supporting global illumination via recursive Monte Carlo path integration. Implements Next Event Estimation (NEE) for direct light sampling at each bounce, and Russian Roulette (RR) termination for unbiased variance reduction with infinite path depth.

StudentAbhinandan Sharma
GPUNVIDIA GTX 1650
StackOptiX 6.5 · CUDA 10.1 · C++
Implementation Highlights
Unidirectional Path Tracing
Paths are traced from the camera, bouncing off surfaces using cosine-weighted hemisphere sampling. Radiance accumulates along the path until termination, capturing full global illumination including color bleeding and indirect shadows.
Next Event Estimation (NEE)
At each path vertex a direct lighting sample is drawn explicitly toward a quad light. A shadow ray confirms visibility and the contribution is added immediately — dramatically reducing variance compared to relying on chance hits to the emitter.
Russian Roulette Termination
Instead of a fixed max depth, paths terminate stochastically with probability proportional to 1 − max channel of throughput. Surviving paths are re-weighted by the inverse probability, keeping the estimator unbiased while enabling infinite path depth (D = ∞).
Progressive Accumulation
Each SPP frame is launched separately and averaged into the result buffer using a running mean, so the image converges progressively rather than requiring all samples in a single kernel launch.
Cosine-Weighted Hemisphere Sampling
Indirect bounce directions are drawn proportional to cos θ, aligning with the Lambertian BRDF. The PDF cancels the cos θ factor in the rendering equation, leaving only the BRDF albedo as the path weight — lower variance than uniform sampling.
Emitter Visibility from Camera
Quad lights have no scene geometry, so the ray generation program checks direct camera-to-light intersections and composites emitter radiance into the result buffer before tracing any surface paths.

New Scene Commands

  • integrator pathtracer
  • spp <N>
  • maxdepth <D> (or infinity)
  • nexteventestimation on|off
  • russianroulette on|off

Implementation Notes

With NEE enabled, emitter hits along indirect paths skip their emission contribution to avoid double-counting the direct light already sampled explicitly. Russian Roulette uses the max RGB channel of the current throughput as the survival probability, clamped to avoid division by near-zero weights.

Extra Credits

Cosine Importance Sampling

Implemented cosine importance sampling for the indirect bounce direction. Rather than drawing directions uniformly from the hemisphere (pdf = 1/2π), samples are drawn proportional to cos θ (pdf = cos θ/π), concentrating paths near the surface normal where the Lambertian response is strongest. The cos θ factor in the rendering equation cancels with the pdf, simplifying the per-bounce weight to π · brdf — lower variance than uniform sampling for diffuse surfaces at no extra cost per sample.

New Scene Command

  • importancesampling cosine

Assignment 4
Multiple Importance Sampling

Extends the path tracer with GGX microfacet BRDFs, BRDF importance sampling for both Phong and GGX, and Multiple Importance Sampling (MIS) combining NEE area sampling and BRDF sampling via the power heuristic — dramatically reducing variance on glossy surfaces illuminated by area lights.

StudentAbhinandan Sharma
GPUNVIDIA GTX 1650
StackOptiX 6.5 · CUDA 10.1 · C++
Implementation Highlights
Cosine Importance Sampling
Indirect bounce directions are drawn proportional to cosθ (pdf = cosθ/π) instead of uniformly from the hemisphere (pdf = 1/2π). The cosine factor in the rendering equation cancels with the pdf, simplifying the per-bounce weight to π · brdf and reducing variance on diffuse surfaces at no extra cost per sample.
GGX Microfacet BRDF
Physically-based specular model using the GGX normal distribution D, Smith masking-shadowing G = G1(wi)·G1(wo) (Schlick approximation), and Schlick Fresnel F. Evaluated as kd/π + F·G·D / (4·cosθᵢ·cosθₒ). Roughness alpha is set per-geometry.
BRDF Importance Sampling
A mixture sampler splits samples between specular and diffuse lobes proportional to t = ksAvg / (kdAvg + ksAvg). Phong specular samples around the mirror direction using a power-cosine distribution; GGX samples a microfacet normal h from the GGX distribution then reflects to get wi. Both use cosine-weighted hemisphere sampling for the diffuse lobe.
Multiple Importance Sampling (MIS)
Direct lighting combines one BRDF sample and one NEE area sample per quad light, weighted by the power heuristic w = p_a² / (p_a² + p_b²). Each strategy's contribution is scaled by its MIS weight, eliminating the high-variance spikes that appear when either strategy is used alone on glossy surfaces.
Power Heuristic Weighting
The power heuristic (β = 2) is used rather than the balance heuristic, as it more aggressively suppresses large-variance samples from the weaker strategy. BRDF and NEE pdfs are evaluated analytically for each other's samples without resampling.
Quad Light Analytical Occlusion
Quad lights have no OptiX geometry, so shadow rays pass through them without triggering anyHit. An additional analytical intersection check tests every shadow ray against all other quad lights, ensuring inter-light shadowing is correctly accounted for.
Unified BRDF / Sampling Pipeline
Both brdfType (Phong or GGX) and importanceSampling mode (hemisphere, cosine, brdf) are stored per-geometry and forwarded through the payload. The same closest-hit shader handles all combinations via branching, with no separate integrators needed.

New Scene Commands

  • brdf phong|ggx
  • roughness <alpha>
  • importancesampling cosine|brdf
  • gamma <value>

Implementation Notes

The GGX specular mixture probability is floored at 0.25 so that at least 25% of samples always probe the specular lobe, preventing degenerate pdfs on near-diffuse surfaces. The NEE pdf is converted from area measure to solid angle as R²/(A·cosθₒ) for each quad light and averaged across all lights for the MIS weight computation.

Starting Point
Baseline · A4 Stanford Bunny — GGX + MIS (Pre-Project) Opaque GGX bunny on a flat grey floor with a single quad area light and black background. This was the milestone render before any final-project features were added.
Objective 1 — Specular Transmission & Medium Tracking
What Was Added
Rays can refract through surfaces using Snell's law with Schlick Fresnel to choose reflection vs transmission. Each ray tracks its current medium IOR on the payload (currentIOR), updated when entering or exiting transmissive geometry — sufficient for the single-shell bunny, with glass shadow rays passing through dielectric surfaces.
How It Works
Glass materials use a dedicated transmissive path: on hit, Fresnel decides reflect or refract, the ray origin is offset to avoid self-intersection, and shadow rays pass through glass without blocking light. This is the foundation for wax — rays must enter the volume before scattering can happen inside.
Obj. 1 · Glass Stanford Bunny — Clear Glass Transmission-only validation render. The bunny refracts background and floor through its surface with Fresnel highlights on curved regions. No volumetric scattering — pure specular transmission.
Objective 2 — Volumetric Light Transport (SSS)
What Was Added
Inside a participating medium, throughput is attenuated by Beer-Lambert absorption. Before each surface intersection, a scatter distance is sampled from an exponential distribution. At scatter events, outgoing directions follow the Henyey-Greenstein phase function.
Wax Material
The wax path refracts rays into the bunny interior, runs multi-scatter volume transport in the ray generation loop, and on exit applies direct lighting plus approximate wrap and back-transmission terms for shell glow. Material parameters (sigmat, sigmas, anisotropy, diffuse colour) control milkiness, warmth, and ear translucency.
Obj. 2 · SSS Stanford Bunny — Subsurface Scattering (Quad Lights) Wax bunny under area lights only, no HDR background. Shows volumetric glow in thin regions (ears, edges) and soft diffuse falloff on the body. Isolates objective 2 from environment lighting.
Objective 3 — HDR Environment Map + Importance Sampling
What Was Added
HDR .hdr files are loaded at startup and a 2D luminance-weighted CDF is built for importance sampling bright regions of the map. Miss rays return environment radiance; at each shading point, environment NEE samples are combined with BRDF samples via the same power-heuristic MIS used in assignment 4. Uniform sphere sampling is available via envuniform on for variance comparisons.
Scene Control
Environment rotation, pitch, intensity, and UV scale let me frame the backdrop — hiding room corners and placing a softbox highlight upper-right in the hero scene. Quad lights supplement the HDR for warm wax colour where the map alone would look too cool or dim.
Obj. 3 · Sky HDR SSS Bunny — Outdoor Sky Environment Subsurface bunny lit primarily by an outdoor HDR sky map. Demonstrates environment-as-background and image-based illumination on a translucent material.
Obj. 3 · Studio HDR SSS Bunny — Studio HDR Environment Same SSS material under an indoor studio HDR with visible softbox in the backdrop. Shows how luminance-weighted sampling and env rotation frame the lighting for a product-shot look.
Objective 4 — Texture Mapping & Tone Mapping
What Was Added
Triangles support UV coordinates with diffuse and normal map textures loaded through OptiX texture samplers. Albedo textures are decoded from sRGB to linear before shading. Normal maps perturb the shading normal with a blend toward the geometric normal to keep highlights stable under HDR lighting.
Tone Mapping
ACES filmic tone mapping, a linear exposure multiplier, and gamma 2.2 encoding are applied in Renderer.cpp when saving the image, compressing HDR path-tracer output into displayable range without crushing shadows or blowing out the wax glow. The hero scene uses this pipeline together with the marble floor texture.
Obj. 4 · Textures + ACES Hero Scene — Marble Floor + ACES Tone Map Final composite: UV-mapped marble tiles on the floor (diffuse + normal maps), ACES tone mapping with tuned exposure, combined with objectives 1–3. This is the same hero image shown at the top of the page.
